Integral Experiment

Preamble

This experiment evaluates the performance of various LLMs on an integral calculus problem across a wide range of temperatures. The aim of the experiment is to determine the quality of mathematical reasoning across a range of temperatures, parameter counts and architectures, as well as identifying potential phase transitions in capability.

Experiment Prompt:
Solve the following integral: ∫((tan(ln(x)))^3)/x dx.

Model Selection

The experiment spans 33 models, ranging from small-scale (1 billion parameters) to frontier-class (671B+ billion parameters) with Dense, Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) and hybrid models. The chosen models were tested on this problem across a temperature range of 0.0 to 2.0.

Temperature Analysis: Solve Rate vs. Temperature

Temperature Affect on Performance

Most high-performing models maintain stability across the 0.0-1.2 temperature range. However, as temperature increases beyond 1.4, a decrease in performance can be observed for several models, indicating a breakdown in logical consistency as randomness increases. This type of problem which requires a long derivation increases the likelihood of mistakes which become increasingly common at high temperatures.

Parameter Count Affect on Performance

While total parameter count is a common metric for model "size," modern Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures only activate a fraction of their parameters during any single inference step. Conclusion

The results demonstrate a general correlation between model scale and mathematical reasoning success, with a phase transition occurring around the 3-4B parameter mark. The temperature has an effect on the performance with many models suffering from a decline in performance beyond a temperature of 1.4.
